Documentation

Résumé

Objectif

On a crée cet outil pour faciliter l'accès aux projections climatique régionales de pointe au grand public. Les données climatiques utilisées sont ouvertement disponibles et les scripts Python développé pour ce site sont basés sur du code source ouvert. Notre contribution est de limiter l'information à quelques graphiques basiques facilement interprétables qui pourront être réutilisé dans des rapports impliquant le changement climatique.

Méthodes

Les graphiques décrites dans présentation des projections climatiques sont basées sur quatre fonctionnalités basiques:

  • Les moyennes de périodes sont utilisées pour la carte de la période de référence et la carte de la projection. Parce que la variabilité inter-annuelle peut dominer le signal du changement climatique, le climat future comme le climat de la période de référence doivent être traités comme moyennes de périodes (nous suggérons 300 années). Pour chaque grille la moyenne temporelle de tout les valeurs mensuelles (de la saison choisie) pour les années comprises dans la période est calculée.
  • Les moyennes régionales sont utilisées pour la trajectoire de la moyenne régionale et pour le cycle annuel de la moyenne régionale. La moyenne régionale es calculé pour chaque pas de temps en tant que la moyenne de toutes les grilles touchés par la région. Les grilles sont pondérées par la fraction de la surface de la région recouverte par la grille.
  • Nous présentons la moyenne de l'ensemble des projections climatiques au lieu de présenter la projection de chaque modèle individuellement. La moyenne de l'ensemble est la moyenne des quatre modèles est elle est calculé pour chaque grille ou pour chaque pas de temps selon la graphique.
  • En montrant l'ensemble de la moyenne uniquement, l'information sur les différences entre les modèles et sur l'incertitude sont perdues. Dans la carte de projection nous montrant le désaccord entre les modèles pour partiellement réintroduire l'information sur l'incertitude. Nous disons qu'il y a un désaccord entre les modèles sur le changement climatique projeté si au moins deux des quatre modèles projettent un changement qui diffère de la moyenne de l'ensemble dans le signe du changement.

Limitations

  • Petit Ensemble: Les projections sont présentées pour un ensemble de quatre modèles climatiques régionaux. Nous soutenons qu'un plus grand ensemble n'améliorerait pas forcement la qualité des projections en raison des biais provenant de l'utilisation répétée des mêmes modèles de circulation générale (voir choix de l'ensemble). Cependant, une comparaison de notre ensemble de quatre modèles avec un plus vaste ensemble augmenterait la crédibilité des projections.
  • Projections de Précipitation: L'incertitude des projections de précipitations est particulièrement élevée pour la plupart de l'Afrique. Cela s'explique en parti par un manque d'un réseau de stations météorologiques dense dans beaucoup de régions africaines (Kim 2014).
  • Performance of models to reproduce extreme events: Although bias correction techniques have been applied to overcome systematic biases found in model simulations, for some regions climate change indicators as hot extremes (TXx) or wet extremes (RX1day) are over- or under estimated by models. See bias correction for more details.

Data Sources

Regional Climate Models

Analysing climate projections on the national scale requires high resolution data, which isn't provided by global circulation models (GCM). For a restricted region high resolution climate projections can be obtained from regional climate models (RCM). RCMs model local impacts of changes in global greenhouse gas concentrations. Therefore RCMs require global climate simulations of GCMs as input. Thus RCM climate projections are always the result of the combination of a GCM with a RCM.

Choice of the ensemble

The studied ensemble includes four combinations of RCMs and GCMs. Although more combinations would be possible, we restrict ourself to a smaller set in which each model appears only once in order to avoid biases towards one of the models.

Choice of the scenario

Climate projections are shown for the emission scenario RCP4.5, which is in between the emission scenario meeting leading to a 2°C global warming above pre-industrial (RCP2.6) and the business as usual scenario (RCP8.5).
Instead of using fixed time periods and different emission scenarios, one can look at time periods for which a certain global warming level above pre-industrial is reached. For each model we estimate the twenty year time period reaching a certain warming level. When presenting the ensemble mean projections for differences between warming levels, different periods are used for the different models:

EWEMBI (observations and reanalysis)

The EWEMBI is a product of several reanalysis and observation datasets (Lange 2016). It has global coverage on a 0.5°x0.5° grid. EWEMBI data is used for bias correction as well as for the representation of the reference climatology. Please find more information on https://www.isimip.org/gettingstarted/details/27/

Bias Correction

A trend-preserving bias correction technique is applied to the RCM climate projections. This bias correction technique adjusts climate simulations to observations over a reference periods without influencing projected trends (Hempel 2013). The EWEMBI dataset (described above) is used as reference for the bias correction (Lange 2016).

For monthly mean temperature and monthly precipitation bias correction largely improves the quality of simulations. The model spread is reduced and the ensemble mean is close to the EWEMBI data. Note that the used bias correction method does not aim at correcting trends towards observations and therefore the difference in trends between EWEMBI and the model data is not related to the bias correction technique but to the limited quality of climate models.

For extreme values as hot extremes (TXx), wet extremes (RX1day) and 5day wet extremes (RX5day) differences between EWEMBI data and model projections remain after bias correction. Bias correction mainly reduces the model spread but partly fails to correct the ensemble mean towards EWEMBI. Shortcomings of the used bias correction method for weather extremes have been reported by Sippel et al. (Sippel 2016).

Climate Indicators

Besides monthly mean temperature and monthly cumulated precipitation three climate change indices can be analysed:

  • Hot extremes TXx, Monthly maximum value of daily maximum temperature:
    Let TXx be the daily maximum temperatures in month k, period j. The maximum daily maximum temperature each month is then: TXxkj=max(TXxkj)
  • Wet extremes Rx1day, Monthly maximum 1-day precipitation:
    Let RRij be the daily precipitation amount on day i in period j. The maximum 1-day value for period j are: Rx1dayj = max (RRij)
  • 5day wet extremes Rx5day, Monthly maximum consecutive 5-day precipitation:
    Let RRkj be the precipitation amount for the 5-day interval ending k, period j. Then maximum 5-day values for period j are: Rx5dayj = max (RRkj)

Please find more information about these indicators on http://etccdi.pacificclimate.org.

In the current version now drought indicators are included. One can draw basic assumptions about changes in drought risk by analysing monthly precipitation changes in combination with changes in monthly mean temperature assuming that potential evapotranspiration increases with higher temperatures.

Presentation of Climate Projections

Reference Period Map

For each indicator, a map of the climatology is presented. This map is a period average over the reference period for the EWEMBI dataset. For temperature indicators, hotter regions are darker than cooler regions. For precipitation indicators, wetter regions are darker than drier regions.

Projection Map

Changes in climate indicators are presented as the difference between the period average of the projection period and the period average of the reference period. These differences are calculated individually for each model. In the map, the ensemble mean change is presented. Grid cells for which the sign of the projected change of two models differs from the the ensemble mean are coloured in grey as models disagree on how the future climate might change in this cell. Changes towards warmer or drier conditions are coloured in red, changes towards cooler or wetter conditions are coloured in blue.

Regionally Averaged Transient

For the transient plots, the regional average is taken at each time step (grid-points are weighted by the fraction of the grid-point belonging to the region). The evolution of this area average is shown for the EWEMBI dataset as well as for RCM projections. The ensemble mean of RCM projections are shown by a thick green line while the model spread is represented by green shading. When choosing a month of the year or a defined season, only the time steps corresponding to the month (months in season) are considered. Results are presented as a 20 year running mean.

Regionally Averaged Annual Cycle

In the upper panel, the annual cycle is shown for the reference period for EWEMBI and RCM simulations. In the lower panel the projected change in the annual cycle is shown. This projected change is computed as the difference between the annual cycle of the projection period and the annual cycle of the reference period. As for the transients the regional average is presented. The ensemble mean of RCM projections are shown by a thick green line while the model spread is represented by green shading.

Climate Data Tool (Python)

This website is based on a climate data tool developed for the analysis of regional climate information. Besides the functions to compute period averages, period averages and ensemble means the tool has several additional that could be useful for for more sophisticated analysis.
The open source code and a documentation of the tool will be published soon.
