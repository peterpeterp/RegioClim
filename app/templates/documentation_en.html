Documentation

Introduction

Purpose

We created this tool to facilitate the access to state of the art regional climate projections to a wider public. The used data is publicly available and the scripts developed for this website are based on open source code. Our contribution here is to constrain the information to some basic graphics that can easily be interpreted and reused for reports involving climate change.

Methods

The four graphics described in presentation of the projections are based on four basic functionalities:

  • Period averages are used in overview maps as the climatology map and the projection map. As inter-annual variability might be larger than the climate change signal the future climate as well as the current climate have to be treated as period averages (we suggest 30 years). For each grid-cell the time average of all monthly values (of the chosen months) for the years within the chosen period is computed.
  • Area averages are used for the transient plot and the annual cycle plot. The area average is computed at each time step as the average of all grid-cells touched by the region. Grid-cells are weighted by the fraction of the area of the region covered by the grid-cell.
  • We display the ensemble mean of the model projections instead of showing the results separately for each model. The ensemble mean is the mean value of the 4 models computed at each grid-cell or at each time step depending on the plot.
  • Only showing the ensemble mean hides information about the discrepancies between models and the uncertainty of the projections. In the projection map we therefore show model disagreement to reintroduce some of the uncertainty information. Here we say that models disagree on the projected change if at least two of the four models project a change that differs in sign from the ensemble mean.

Limitations

  • Small Ensemble: Model projections are shown for the ensemble mean of 4 regional climate models. The choice of the ensemble is documented in ensemble choice.
  • Precipitation Projections: There is a high uncertainty concerning precipitation projections in most parts of Africa. This a partly due to the sparse network of meteorological stations in most parts of Africa. Observations are required for model calibration
  • Performance of models to reproduce extreme events: Although bias correction techniques have been applied to overcome systematic biases found in model simulations, for some regions climate change indicators as hot extremes (TXx) or wet extremes (RX1day) are over- or under estimated by models. See bias correction for more details.

Data Sources

Regional Climate Models

Analysing climate projections on the national scale requires high resolution data, which isn't provided by global climate models (GCM). Regional climate models (RCM) provide high resolution climate projections for a restricted region. RCMs model local impacts of changes in global greenhouse gas concentrations. Therefore RCMs require global climate simulations of GCMs as input. Thus RCM climate projections are always the result of the combination of a GCM with a RCM.

Choice of the ensemble

The studied ensemble includes four combinations of RCMs and GCMs. Although more combinations would be possible, we restrict ourself to a smaller set in which each model appears only once in order to avoid biases towards one of the models.

Choice of the scenario

Climate projections are shown for the emission scenario RCP4.5, which is in between the emission scenario meeting leading to a 2°C global warming (RCP2.6) and the buisness as susual scenario (RCP8.5).
Instead of using fixed time periods and different emission scnearios, one can look at time periods for which a certain global warming level is reached. For each model we estimate the twenty year time period reaching a certain warming level. When presenting the ensemble mean projections for differences between warming levels, different periods are used for the different models:

Observations (EWEMBI)

The EWEMBI is a product of several reanalysis and observation datasets (Lange 2016). It has global coverage on a 0.5°x0.5° grid. EWEMBI data is used for bias correction as well as for the representation of the reference climatology.

Bias Correction

A trend-preserving bias correction technique is applied to the RCM climate projections. This bias correction technique adjusts climate simulations to observations over a reference periods without influencing projected trends (Hempel 2013). The EWEMBI dataset (described above) is used as reference for the bias correction (Lange 2016).

The following figures show the results of bias correction for Senegal. Non-bias corrected regional climate model data is shown in grey, bias corrected regional climate model data in magenta and bias corrected general circulation model data from ISIMIP in green and EWEMBI in black. As for the transient plots the ensemble mean is represented by the thick line while the ensemble spread is shown by the shading. Th data has been smoothed with a 20 year running mean.

Climate Indicators

Besides monthly mean temperature and monthly cumulated precipitation three climate change indices can be analysed:

  • TXx, Monthly maximum value of daily maximum temperature:
    Let TXx be the daily maximum temperatures in month k, period j. The maximum daily maximum temperature each month is then: TXxkj=max(TXxkj)
  • Rx1day, Monthly maximum 1-day precipitation:
    Let RRij be the daily precipitation amount on day i in period j. The maximum 1-day value for period j are: Rx1dayj = max (RRij)
  • Rx5day, Monthly maximum consecutive 5-day precipitation:
    Let RRkj be the precipitation amount for the 5-day interval ending k, period j. Then maximum 5-day values for period j are: Rx5dayj = max (RRkj)

Please find more information about these indicators on http://etccdi.pacificclimate.org.

Presentation of Climate Projections

Climatology Map

For each indicator, a map of the climatology is presented. This map is a period average over the reference period of the EWEMBI dataset. For temperature indicators, hotter regions are darker than cooler regions. For precipitation indicators, wetter regions are darker than drier regions.

Projection Map

Changes in climate indicators are presented as the difference between the period average of the projection period and the period average of the reference period. These differences are calculated individually for each model. In the map, the ensemble mean change is presented. Grid cells for which the sign of the projected change of two models differs from the the ensemble mean are coloured in grey as models disagree on how the future climate might change in this cell. Changes towards warmer or drier conditions are coloured in red, changes towards cooler or wetter conditions are coloured in blue.

Transients

For the transient plots, the regional average is taken at each time step (grid-points are weighted by the fraction of the grid-point belonging to the region). The evolution of this area average is shown for the EWEMBI dataset as well as for RCM projections. The ensemble mean of RCM projections are shown by a thick green line while the model spread is represented by green shading. When choosing a month of the year or a defined season, only the time steps corresponding to the month (months in season) are considered. Results are presented as a 20 year running mean.

Annual Cycle

In the upper panel, the annual cycle is shown for the reference period for EWEMBI and RCM simulations. In the lower panel the projected change in the annual cycle is shown. This projected change is computed as the difference between the annual cycle of the projection period and the annual cycle of the reference period. As for the transients the regional average is presented. The ensemble mean of RCM projections are shown by a thick green line while the model spread is represented by green shading.

Climate Data Tool (Python)

This website is based on a climate data tool developed for the analysis of regional climate information. The core functionality of the tool is to compute area-averages from gridded data. For each grid-cell, the tool computes the fraction of the grid-cell area which lies within a certain region. This fraction can be used as weighting factor for area averages. Additionally, several useful functionalities such as plot routines, evaluation of model agreement, possibility to merge regions, seasonal averages etc. are included.
We encourage the user to use this tool for more sophisticated analysis. The open source code and a documentation of the tool can be found on github/wo_denn?/
